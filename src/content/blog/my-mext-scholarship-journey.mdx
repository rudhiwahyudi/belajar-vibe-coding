This year I started preparing my application for the **MEXT (Monbukagakusho) scholarship** — a Japanese government scholarship for international graduate students. This post is the first in a series documenting that journey: why I'm doing it, what the process looks like, and what I'm learning along the way.

## Why MEXT, and why now

After a few years working as a software and DevOps engineer, I kept running into the same wall: the systems I was building worked, but I didn't fully understand *why* certain distributed systems patterns held up under load and others didn't. I wanted to study these problems more rigorously, with access to research labs working on resilient, large-scale infrastructure.

MEXT stood out because it:

- Fully covers tuition and provides a living stipend, removing financial barriers to research
- Has a strong track record of placements in systems and infrastructure labs
- Requires a research proposal upfront, which forced me to clarify my own research direction early

## What my research proposal looks like

My current proposal direction is **resilient orchestration for multi-cloud Kubernetes deployments** — drawing directly from problems I hit while building [CloudOps Dashboard](/projects/cloudops-dashboard). Specifically, I'm interested in:

1. How control planes can detect and recover from partial cloud-provider outages
2. Cost-aware scheduling across heterogeneous cloud regions
3. Formal models for verifying rollout safety in GitOps pipelines

## How I'm preparing

A few concrete things I'm doing in parallel:

- **Japanese language study** — currently working through N4-level material, aiming for conversational basics before departure
- **Reaching out to potential supervisors** — reading recent papers from a few labs and drafting short, specific introduction emails
- **Documenting my engineering work** — writing up project case studies (like the ones on this site) to support the "research potential" part of the application
